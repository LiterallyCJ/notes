## Substitution

The method of substitution is the integral version of the Chain Rule. Remember that,
$$
\frac{d}{dx} (f(g(x))) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)
$$

so integrating, we get
$$
\int f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\ dx = f(g(x)) + c
$$
We can rewrite this using $u = g(x)$. Then $\frac{du}{dx} = g'(x)$, or loosely, $du = g'(x)\ dx$. So we get,
$$
\begin{align}
\int f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\ dx 
&= \int f'(u)\ du \text{ substituting } g'(x)\ dx \text{ for } du  \\
&= f(u) + c \\
&= f(g(x)) + c
\end{align}
$$

ex.

Find $\int e^x \sqrt{1 + e^x}\ dx$

Let $u = 1+e^x$, then $du = e^x dx$
Now we have, 
$$
\begin{align}
\int \sqrt{u}\ du 
&= \int u^{\frac{1}{2}} \\
&= \frac{2}{3}u^{\frac{3}{2}} + c \\
&= \frac{2}{3}(1 + e^x)^{\frac{3}{2}} + c
\end{align}
$$

ex.
Find the definite integral
$$
\int^8_0 \frac{\cos \sqrt{x + 1}}{\sqrt{x + 1}}
$$
Let $u = \sqrt{x + 1}$. Then by chain rule, $\frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x + 1}}$, and therefore $2du = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x + 1}} \cdot dx$ 
We also need to change the bounds to be in terms of $u$ rather than in terms of $x$. So, we need to use $u = \sqrt{x + 1}$, when $x = 0$, $u = 1$ and when $x = 8$, $u = 3$
