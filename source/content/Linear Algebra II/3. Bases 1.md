
### Spanning

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $F$. Let $x_1, \dots, x_n \in V$.

1. An element $x \in V$ is called a linear combination of $x_1, \dots, x_n$ if there are $a_1, \dots, a_n$ such that $x = a_1x_1 + \dots + a_nx_n$
2. The subset of $V$ consisting of all linear combinations of $x_1, \dots, x_n$ is called the span of $x_1, \dots, x_n$ and is denoted by $Span(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ or $Span_F(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ i.e
$$
Span(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \{a_1x_1 + \dots + a_nx_n | a_1, \dots, a_n \in F\}
$$
3. We say that $x_1, \dots, x_n$ span $V$ or that $x_1, \dots, x_n$ form a spanning set of $V$ if $V = Span(x_1, \dots, x_n)$, or every $x \in V$ is a linear combination of $x_1, \dots, x_n$.

A linear combination of $x_1, \dots, x_n$ is any vector of the form
$$
a_1x_1 + \dots + a_nx_n
$$
where $a_1, \dots, a_n$ are scalars from the field $F$

> A linear combination is when you multiply every vector in the space by a scalar and add them together

>A span is a subset of $V$ which contains all linear combinations, basically every number you can make from a subset

> A spanning set of $V$ is a subset where the linear combinations in the subset can form every vector in $V$

### Linear Independence

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $F$. Let $x_1, \dots, x_n \in V$. We say that $x_1, \dots, x_n$ are linearly independent over $F$ if the following conditions hold:
$$
\begin{gather*}
\text{if } a_1, \dots, a_n \in F \text{ and } a_1x_1+ \dots + a_nx_n = 0_V\\
\text{then } a_1 = \dots = a_n = 0_F
\end{gather*}
$$
Otherwise they are linearly dependent.

> This basically means that the only way to make $0_V$ is to have all of the scalars be 0.

#### Proving linear independence

In $\mathbb{R}^2$:
Let
$$
\begin{gather*}
x_1 = \pmatrix{1 \\ 0} \\
x_2 = \pmatrix{0 \\ 1}
\end{gather*}
$$

To prove linear independency, assume
$$
a_1x_1 + a_2x_2 = 0
$$
Then
$$
\begin{gather*}
a_1 \pmatrix{1 \\ 0} + a_2 \pmatrix{0 \\ 1} = \pmatrix{0 \\ 0}
\end{gather*}
$$
So
$$
\pmatrix{a_1 \\ a_2} = \pmatrix{0 \\ 0}
$$
Hence
$$
a_1 = 0, a_2 = 0
$$

Therefore the only solution is the trivial solution and $x_1$ and $x_2$ are linearly independent

We could also turn it into a system of equations
In $\mathbb{R}^3$:

Suppose:
$$
\begin{gather*}
x_1 = \pmatrix{1 \\ 2 \\ 3} \\
x_2 = \pmatrix{-2 \\ 1 \\ 0} \\
x_3 = \pmatrix{1 \\ 0 \\ 1}
\end{gather*}
$$
Now assume,
$$
a_1x_1 + a_2x_2 + a_3x_3 = 0
$$
Then
$$
a_1 \pmatrix{1 \\ 2 \\ 3} + a_2 \pmatrix{-2 \\ 1 \\ 0} + a_3 \pmatrix{1 \\ 0 \\ 1} = \pmatrix{0 \\ 0 \\ 0}
$$

Comparing coordinates gives:
$$
\begin{align}
&a_1 &- &2a_2 &+ &a_3 &= 0 \\
&2a_1 &+ &1a_2 & & &= 0 \\
&3a_1 & & &+ &a_3 &= 0 \\
\end{align}
$$
Now solving the system we get:
$$
a_1 = a_2 = a_3 = 0
$$

Finally, we could use the vectors as column vectors in a matrix and row reduce to prove it is dependent by having no free variables. If all variables are forced to be 0, then the vector is linearly independent
### Bases

Let $V$ be a vector space over field $F$. Let $x_1, \dots, x_n \in V$. We say that $x_1, \dots, x_n$ form a basis of $V$ if $x_1, \dots, x_n$ both span $V$ and are linearly independent.
