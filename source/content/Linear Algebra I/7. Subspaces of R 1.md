A subset $V$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is said to be a subspace if the three conditions hold:
1. $V \neq \emptyset$ ($V$ is non-empty)
2. for all $\vec{u}, \vec{v} \in V$, $\vec{u} + \vec{v} \in V$ ($V$ is closed under addition)
3. for all $\vec{u} \in V$ and all $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$, $\lambda \vec{u} \in V$ (V is closed under multiplication by scalars)

ex.
- $\mathbb{R}^n$ itself is a subspace

- The zero subspace $V_0 = \{\vec{0}\} \in \mathbb{R}^n$
	1. $V_0 \neq \emptyset$
	2. if $\vec{u}, \vec{v} \in V_0$, then $\vec{u} = \vec{v} = \vec{0}$, so $\vec{u} + \vec{v} = \vec{0} \in V_0$
	3. if $\vec{u} \in V$, $\vec{u} = \vec{0}$, if $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$, $\lambda \cdot \vec{u} = \vec{0} \in V_0$

- Let $\Pi$ be a plane passing through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Then $\Pi$ has a Cartesian equation $ax + by + cz = 0$ for some $a,\ b,\ c \in \mathbb{R}$ (not all 0). Thus $\Pi$ can be identified with the set of position vectors $V = \pmatrix{x \\ y \\ z} \in \mathbb{R}^3 | ax + by + cz = 0$
## Null spaces

Let $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be a linear transformation. Then the null space of $T$ is the subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ defined by:
$$
N(T) = \{\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n | T(x) = \vec{0} \in \mathbb{R}^m\}
$$

(Basically the set of all matrices in $\mathbb{R}^n$ which turn to 0 when multiplied by any matrix $T$)
